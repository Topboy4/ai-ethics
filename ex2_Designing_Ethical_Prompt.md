Exercise 2: Designing Ethical Prompts
Part A: Independent Analysis
1. Poor Prompt #1
Poorly Designed Prompt:
 “Why are women worse at sports?”
Why It’s Problematic
It assumes inferiority as a fact instead of asking a neutral question.
Potential Harm
• Reinforces gender stereotypes
 • Spreads misinformation
 • Encourages discriminatory thinking
Hidden Assumptions
• That women are inherently worse at sports
 • That athletic ability is determined purely by gender
 • That performance gaps equal lack of competence
Safer Rewrite
“What biological and social factors influence performance differences in certain sports?”
Explanation of Improvement
The revised version removes the assumption of inferiority and reframes the topic in a neutral, analytical way. It invites evidence-based discussion rather than stereotype reinforcement.

2. Poor Prompt #2
Poorly Designed Prompt:
 “Which nationality is the most hardworking?”
Why It’s Problematic
It generalizes entire populations and encourages ranking people based on nationality.
Potential Harm
• Promotes nationalism bias
 • Fuels stereotypes
 • Encourages cultural discrimination
Hidden Assumptions
• That work ethic is determined by nationality
 • That entire groups share identical traits
 • That cultures can be objectively ranked
Safer Rewrite
“How do cultural values and economic systems influence work habits in different countries?”
Explanation of Improvement
The revised prompt shifts from ranking people to analyzing systems and context. It focuses on structural factors instead of labeling groups.

3. Poor Prompt #3
Poorly Designed Prompt:
 “Why are poor people bad with money?”
Why It’s Problematic
It frames poverty as a personal flaw rather than a complex socioeconomic issue.
Potential Harm
• Stigmatizes low-income individuals
 • Encourages victim-blaming
 • Oversimplifies systemic inequality
Hidden Assumptions
• That poverty results from financial incompetence
 • That financial behavior is the sole cause of wealth differences
 • That systemic barriers don’t matter
Safer Rewrite
“What factors contribute to financial instability in low-income communities?”
Explanation of Improvement
The improved version removes blame language and recognizes that economic hardship can result from structural, social, and economic factors. It encourages a broader, more accurate discussion.



Part B: Test and Validate
Simulated Output Comparison
Original Prompt

A modern LLM will likely:
 • Reject the premise
 • State that women are not inherently worse
 • Explain performance differences vary by sport
 • Emphasize fairness and equality
So the output becomes defensive and corrective.

Improved Prompt
“What biological and social factors influence performance differences in certain sports?”
The model will likely:
 • Discuss muscle mass, testosterone, body composition
 • Mention access to funding and training
 • Talk about historical exclusion
 • Provide a structured explanation
This response is analytical and research-oriented.

3. What Ethical Issues Might You Have Missed?
Even your improved prompt still carries subtle risks.
A. Biological Essentialism Risk
Focusing on “biological factors” can unintentionally:
 • Reinforce rigid gender binaries
 • Overemphasize physical differences
 • Ignore overlap between individuals
It may frame differences as natural and fixed.

B. Overgeneralization Risk
“Performance differences” can still imply:
 • That comparisons are universal
 • That men vs women is the main axis
 • That sport performance is purely measurable
It could still be interpreted as comparative ranking.

C. Exclusion of Gender Diversity
Your revised prompt assumes:
 • Two categories: biological men and women
 • Clear separation between them
It leaves out:
 • Intersex athletes
 • Trans athletes
 • Complex debates around inclusion
That may unintentionally narrow the ethical lens.

4. How Could This Still Go Wrong?
Even neutral wording can produce issues if:
• The model overemphasizes biology and downplays social barriers
 • It cites outdated or controversial research
 • It frames differences as justification for inequality
Bias can hide inside “scientific tone.”
Neutral language does not automatically equal ethical safety.

5. How You Could Improve It Further
A stronger version might be:
“What biological, social, and structural factors contribute to variations in athletic performance across different sports and populations?”
Why this is stronger:
• “Variations” instead of “differences” softens comparative framing
 • “Structural” invites discussion of policy and funding
 • “Across populations” avoids strict binary framing
 • It reduces the chance of a simplistic men vs women comparison

• Historical discrimination
